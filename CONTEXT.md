# TechPersona Studio — context glossary

A shared glossary for the website work. Definitions only, no implementation detail.
Update inline as terms get resolved during planning.

---

## Artifacts

**Marketing site (v3)**
The agency's own front door. The page cold strangers land on. Its job is to make a
visitor from any service business feel understood and request first contact. Built
once, serves every vertical. This is what `techpersona_studio_v3.html` is.

**Proposal (the "OM")**
A tailored one-page pitch sent to a single warm prospect *after* a call, written
entirely in that prospect's language (e.g. the Quan Pho / pho-restaurant proposal).
Closes a specific deal. A separate tool from the Marketing site — not the same artifact,
not the same job.

---

## Audience

**Service business owner**
The reader of the Marketing site. Could run a restaurant, a weight-loss coaching
practice, a clinic, a trades company. Burned by agencies, allergic to jargon. The site
targets all of them at once by naming pain they all share, anchored with concrete,
switchable examples — never faceless "we help businesses grow" copy.

**Speak their language (on the Marketing site)**
Naming the universal pain so sharply, with such concrete examples, that a reader from
any vertical thinks "that's me." Achieved through vividness, not by narrowing to one
industry. (Industry-narrow language belongs in the Proposal, not here.)

**The "aha, that's me" test**
Every line should make the reader think *"ah, yes — I have that problem, and I want this
person to fix it"* and read on. Copy earns its place by triggering recognition plus desire
to keep reading; if it doesn't, it's padding.

---

## Voice

**Founder voice**
The Marketing site speaks first-person as the founder ("I'm Thao"), under the studio
brand name "TechPersona Studio." A named human earns a stranger's trust faster than a
faceless "we" when there's no track record yet. Scales to "we" once a team exists.

---

## The offer & funnel

**Website Audit** (the front-door offer)
Free. The visitor sends only their URL; the founder sends back a short Loom video plus
written leaks within ~2 business days. The single primary CTA across the whole Marketing
site. Zero-friction because it needs no call and no calendar.

**Opportunity Map Audit** (the call payoff)
Free, but only works *after* the first call, because it needs the owner's input. Pinpoints
the real operational bottleneck (lead leaking, scaling issues, etc.). Teased on the site as
what you'll uncover *together on the call* — a reason to take the call, never the front-door
offer.

*Customer-facing name:* **"your biggest win."** "Opportunity Map" is internal only — it's
jargon an owner won't parse. On the site this step reads as "we find your biggest win: your
single biggest bottleneck and the one fix that pays you back fastest." A named, tangible
takeaway is the reason to book the call.

**The funnel**
Cold visitor → request Website Audit (URL only) → Loom + leaks earn the call → on the call,
deliver the Opportunity Map → leads into the tailored Proposal (the OM).

**Scheduling link** (warm leads only)
A private book-a-call link sent in direct follow-up (text, email, DM). Never a public CTA
on the Marketing site. Used when someone is already warm and ready to talk — not for cold
strangers self-serving a slot off the homepage.

**Warm-lead routing** (split by signal)
Warm leads do not all skip the audit. Route by what they signal:
- **Ready to talk** (e.g. referral who says "let's chat") → scheduling link immediately.
- **Wants help with the site** (e.g. "can you look at my website?") → audit first, same
  as cold traffic; scheduling link only after the Loom earns the call.

**Risk-free framing (steps 1 + 2)**
Both free steps — the Website Audit *and* the call where we find your biggest win — carry
zero obligation; the owner can walk after either, owing nothing. Stated **once**, with
weight, in the process section ("Steps 1 and 2 are free. If you don't see the value, walk
away — nothing owed."). Do *not* sprinkle "free / no obligation" across every section;
repeated five times it reads as defensive and salesy. One clear, well-placed reassurance.

## The two universal pains

**Lead** — leads the owner already paid to attract leak away to competitors before
anyone talks to them. A profit leak.

**Scale** — as the business grows, repetitive, boring, time-consuming work buries the
owner and their staff, so they can't focus on high-value work or quality relationships.

---

## The three payoffs (pillars)

These are conceptual pillars, **not a standalone repeated section.** Each payoff is
expressed *inside* its matching automation card (profit/time/relationships), so the
reader meets the benefit once, attached to the thing that delivers it. A separate
"here's what that buys you" section re-states the cards back-to-back and reads as
salesy padding — avoid it.


1. **Stop the profit leak** — keep the customers (and money) currently walking to competitors.
2. **Hours back** — repetitive busywork runs itself, so the owner and staff get their time back.
3. **Better relationships** — customers feel looked-after, so loyalty and results improve, not
   just time saved.

---

## One path, two halves (the throughline)

The whole site hangs from a single story: **one stranger, carried all the way to a
loyal customer.** That path has two halves:

- **Website** = the *front half* — how a stranger finds and trusts you.
- **Automation** = the *back half* — how you catch and keep them once they reach out.

Most owners only have the front half (or a broken version of it), so they leak
everyone in the gap.

**Connected story, modular offer.** The path is a *narrative device* — it shows the
owner the whole picture so they see their leak could be anywhere along it. It is NOT a
forced bundle. The two services are independently buyable: an owner can get the website
*or* the automation alone, whichever matches their situation. Earlier framing ("I build
the whole path, never sell them separately") was wrong — do not imply an all-or-nothing
package.

**Two jobs at once.** The story must connect the halves (so they don't feel disconnected),
*and* the offer must make the two services unmistakably clear and separately purchasable
(so the owner knows exactly what they can buy). Connected in the telling, distinct in the buying.

**Neutral entry, leak decides.** The site does not steer owners to a default starting
half. Their single biggest leak decides where they start. (In practice the website is the
easier wedge that gets people in the door, but the *copy* stays neutral and lets the pain
choose.)

---

## The two services (the *how*, framed as solutions to pain)

**Converting website**
A site that matches the owner's brand (never AI-generated and tasteless), gets found on
Google *and* AI search (content written for humans and AI), and turns visitors into
customers. Serves mainly the *Lead* pain → *stop the profit leak*.

**Automation** (always described as customer outcomes, never as tech)
Three featured outcomes, one per pillar:
- **Never miss another customer** — every missed call/inquiry gets an instant reply, routed
  to the right person. [profit]
- **Booked while you sleep** — after-hours inquiries become booked jobs, hands-off. [time]
- **Customers who feel remembered** — personal, automatic follow-ups and check-ins that
  deepen loyalty. [relationships]

---

## Hide the technology

The Marketing site never shows machinery: no workflow/node diagrams, no tool names, no
"CRM/automation/n8n" vocabulary, no invented result stats (e.g. the old fake "52s"). Tech
stays invisible; only the owner's pain and the customer's experience are shown.

**Customer-journey strip** (the only "flow" allowed)
A simple left-to-right story of what the *customer* experiences (e.g. 8pm inquiry → instant
reply → booked → shows up happy). Reads as outcome, not engineering.

---

## Proof (honest, no client results yet)

Built only from real assets: real before/after redesigns shown as "my work," and polished
spec builds shown as "sample builds / what I deliver." Neither claims a client outcome.
Automation is shown as *what it does*, never *what it achieved* — no fabricated metrics.
